I study the causes and mitigation of intergroup violence in pastoral East Africa. My dissertation identifies the causal mechanisms linking climate shocks to livestock raiding using 40 seasons of household panel data across Kenya and Ethiopia, and tests a behavioral media intervention in a large-scale cluster-RCT across 246 communities. My research spans climate-conflict mechanisms, index insurance, and edutainment.

PhD candidate at Wageningen University & Research. Visiting researcher hosted by Prof. Edward Miguel at the Department of Economics, UC Berkeley, Spring 2026. Member of the World Bank DRIVE evaluation team.

Research

Working Paper — Under Review

Weathering Conflict: Resource Shocks, Insurance, and Pastoral Raiding

N.D. Jensen, J.D. Lopez-Rivas, K. Morsink & E.E. Rikken

40-season household panel of 1,586 households across Kenya and Ethiopia, matched to NASA MODIS satellite pasture data. Identifies two distinct causal pathways from climate shocks to livestock raiding and evaluates the conflict effects of Index-Based Livestock Insurance (IBLI), now scaled through the World Bank’s $365M DRIVE program.

Scarcity pathway: bad pasture forces risky migration, accounting for 73% of concurrent conflict exposure.

Abundance pathway: good rains increase dry-season raiding by 86% through livestock value appreciation.

Insurance reduces scarcity-driven raiding by 57% when payouts trigger — but increases victimization risk by 35% during abundant seasons, as productive investments create visible wealth at the moment defensive capacity is lowest.

In Progress

Conflict in the Drylands: Descriptive Evidence from Pastoral East Africa

K. Morsink, E.E. Rikken, H.H. Son & M. Sugastti

Uses the DRIVE panel dataset — 7,807 respondents across 4,690 households in Kenya and Ethiopia — to document the prevalence, seasonality, and correlates of livestock raiding. Tests the dominant economic theories of conflict (rapacity, opportunity cost, grievance) against household-level evidence, and compares findings against aggregate databases (ACLED, UCDP) to quantify structural undercounting of pastoral violence.

Data Collection Complete — Analysis Ongoing

Edutainment to Mitigate Inter-Group Violence

E.E. Rikken, T.G. Gidey, N.D. Jensen, K. Morsink & K. Shikuku

Cluster-RCT across 246 communities in six Kenyan counties (130 treatment, 116 control), testing a 14-minute animated film and structured deliberation protocol cross-randomized with index insurance. The intervention promotes skills-based employment during low-demand pastoral seasons, models grazing and restitution agreements, and amplifies women’s voices on raiding’s human costs.

Saturation design — 30% vs. 70% treated sublocations within matched clusters — identifies general equilibrium and spillover effects beyond direct exposure.

Baseline December 2024 · Intervention July–August 2025 · Endline December 2025. Supported by IPA, NWO, and World Bank grants.

Kinship-Based versus Age-Set Social Structures and Risk Sharing

E.E. Rikken, E. Bulte & D. van Soest

Lab-in-the-field experiment (Ethiopia, October 2025) comparing how kinship-based and age-set social structures affect informal wealth-sharing, inequality, and risk-pooling behavior after climate shocks.
